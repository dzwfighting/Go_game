Go Overview

According to legend, Go was invented by Emperor Yao around 2300 BC. His son Danzhu was proud and self-satisfied, irritable and willful, so Emperor Yao invented Go to cultivate his son's character.

Go has two kinds of pieces, black and white. It is stipulated that the player holding the black piece goes first, and the two players alternately place black and white pieces at the intersection points on the chessboard grid of nineteen by nineteen lines. After the move is over, you can't regret the move. In the process of playing chess, encircle the land to capture the stones, and the size of the encircled "land" determines the outcome.

The rules of Go are simple and elegant, but the gameplay is ever-changing. It takes a lot of practice and study to master its connotations. Chess master Emmanuel Lasker praised: "If there are other intelligent life forms in the universe, they will almost certainly know Go." At the same time, Go is considered to be one of the most complicated board games in the world. The complexity was proved to be PSPACE-hard by Robertson and Munro in 1978.

As of mid-2008, there were more than 40 million gamers worldwide, the vast majority of them in East Asia. As of the end of 2020, the International Go Federation has 77 member countries and 5 association members.

Modern Go:

After the Meiji Restoration, chess players lost the support of the shogunate and began to seek new means of earning a living, which led to the emergence of news chess games and the modern rank system, and the establishment of a nationwide Japanese chess academy. During the Showa era, Wu Qingyuan and Kigu Mino jointly set off the trend of "new layout" and started the era of modern Go. Afterwards, first-class chess players in the Japanese chess world emerged in large numbers, such as Sakata Eiko, Fujisawa Hideyuki, Takakawa Ge, and later Otake Hideo, Ishida Yoshio, Takemiya Masaki, Kobayashi Koichi and so on.

In 1989, Korean Cao Xunxuan won the first Ing's Cup World Go Championship, which also triggered an upsurge of Go in Korea. Since then, a large number of world chess games have appeared. In these chess battles, South Korean Lee Chang-ho stood out from many chess players and became the first player in the chess world at that time. After 2006, Lee Chang-ho's state declined, and cutting-edge forces such as Lee Se-dol and Coulee began to attack him. The next few years will be the era of Gu Li and Li Shidol's two heroes fighting for hegemony.

In the 2010s, many Chinese chess players rose up. Young chess players such as Jiang Weijie, Fan Tingyu, Shi Yue, Mi Yuting, Chen Yaoye, Zhou Ruiyang, Tan Xiao and so on won the world championships one after another.

Go culture:

In the process of spreading Go for thousands of years, many terms have gradually been produced. Some of them have already entered the popular vocabulary, such as "finale" and so on. Almost every step of Go is expressed in related terms, and it is even possible to completely describe a local battle in words. For example: Black occupies the star position in the upper right corner, Bai Xiaofei hangs, black two places high clip, white one jumps, black follows and jumps, white Xiaofei enters the corner, black three three blocks... This passage uses Go terms to vividly describe common fixed stones journey. Common terms are:

  • Called eating: When a chess piece has only one breath, it is called hitting and eating. Also known as "calling to eat".
  • Qi: At the empty intersections closely adjacent to the chess pieces on the chessboard, the number of Qi of a chess piece cannot exceed four Qi, but the connected chess pieces may have more than four Qi. It is called "tight air" to place a piece tightly on the way of the opponent's piece.
  • Mention: The airless chess pieces will be lifted and taken away from the chessboard. Grabbing the opponent's pieces is called "grabbing".
  • Mesh: On the chessboard, the blank intersection surrounded by one piece is called "mesh".
  • Land: The sum of the number of meshes surrounded by live chess and the live chess itself is called "land".
  • Empty: The area surrounded by chess pieces.
  • Jie: The situation where both sides can take turns to extract the opponent's pieces. The rules of Go stipulate that when robbing, the player who is extracted cannot bring it back directly, and must find robbing materials in other places so that the opponent can make a move before taking it back.
