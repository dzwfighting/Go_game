Korea Chess Academy (Korean: 한국기원) is the management organization of Korean professional Go. Its history can be traced back to the Seoul Chess Academy founded by Zhao Nanzhe in November 1945.

Zhao Nanzhe went to Japan in 1937 to learn from Go player Kiya Mikyudan. In 1945, he returned to China and founded the Chess Academy. Koreans traditionally use the patrol method to open the market. After opening the Korean Chess Academy, Zhao persuaded the Koreans to open the game with a white plate. In 1956, the national hand game was established, and the game of Go was promoted among the people.

Well-known professional chess players:

Zhao Nanzhe

Zhao Nanzhe (Korean: 조남철, November 30, 1923-July 2, 2006), Korean Go professional nine-dan, the father of modern Korean Go.

In 1946, Zhao Nanzhe founded the Hanseong Chess Institute, and Korean professional Go officially appeared. In 1950, under the organization of Zhao Nanzhe, South Korea held the modern Go competition for the first time. Zhao was designated as the third dan, and the others were designated as the first dan. In 1955, Zhao persuaded the President of South Korea, Syngman Rhee, to organize a delegation of professional chess players to go to Taiwan to compete in an international competition with local Go players and win. This victory boosted the low morale of the Korean people, and also made Zhao and Hyundai Go famous. Soon, professional chess games in South Korea were established one after another, and the Seoul Chess Academy was rebuilt in 1968 and renamed the Korean Chess Academy. Zhao himself dominated the Korean chess world for a long time after the 1950s, until the junior chess player Kim In returned from studying in Japan and lost his dominance. In 1983, he was awarded the 9th Dan. In 1994, he entered the National Chess Competition and became the oldest chess player to enter the professional chess competition. With his support, his nephew Zhao Zhixun went to Japan to study and later became the number one chess player in Japan.

Cao Xunxuan

Cao Xunxuan (조훈현, born on March 10, 1953), a famous Korean Go player, a professional 9th dan at the Korean Chess Academy, a nine-time world champion, the first generation founder of modern Korean Go, and is honored as the "Go Emperor".

Cao Xunxuan is the master of Lee Changho, the world champion record holder of Go, known as "Cao Li Master and Apprentice"; the two, together with Liu Changhe and Xu Bongsu, are also known as the "Four Kings of Korea". The chess world has opened up absolute domination. Cao was born in Mokpo City, South Jeolla Province, and entered the dan in 1962, becoming the youngest professional chess player in the history of the Korean Chess Academy (9 years and 7 months). In 1963, Cao went to Japan to learn chess, became a student of Ken Segoshi, and re-entered the dan in Japan. In 1972, he was recognized as a 5th dan by the Japanese Ki Academy. Wang Che Minzhu has a deep friendship in the army. After retiring from the army, he was originally scheduled to return to Japan, but he chose to stay because of fierce confrontation with Seo Bong-soo's 9th Dan. After that, Cao Xunxuan won the hegemony with Seo Bong-soo for many years and became the overlord of Korean chess. In 1989, Cao defeated the Chinese chess player Nie Weiping 9th Dan 3-2 in the first Yingshi Cup and won the championship. This set off a Go upsurge in Korea and became a Korean national hero.

Lee Chang-ho

The second-generation master of Go in Korea is a great chess player second only to Wu Qingyuan in the history of modern Go, or "Go in the 20th century, before Wu Qingyuan, and later there was Lee Chang-ho.

In 1996, the Korea Chess Academy granted him and Liu Changhyuk the promotion from the seventh dan to the ninth dan. He has won all Korean domestic championships and world chess grand slams. Its active period in the international chess world was from 1992 to 2012, and the period from 1996 to 2006 was recognized as the "Lee Chang-ho Era" in the chess world. Lee Sedol
